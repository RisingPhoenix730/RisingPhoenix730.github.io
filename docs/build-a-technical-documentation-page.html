Introduction

Legend of the Five Rings, or "L5R", was a collectible card game, or "CCG" released in 1995, and discontinued in 2015. The game was owned by Alderac Entertainment Group.

A Collectible card game is one similar to Magic: The Gathering, Pokemon, or Hearthstone, in that cards are collected from randomly assorted packs, as opposed to all available cards being provided with a base set.

What you need

In order to play L5R, you will need a few things:

  • Two 40 card decks, Dynasty and Fate.
  • A Stronghold card
  • Some way of keeping track of numbers for Honor and Dishonor, i.e. pen and paper.
The Types of Cards

There are three types of cards in L5R, Dynasty cards, Fate cards, and Strongholds.

Dynasty Cards are distinguished by the black background color on the back face of the card. These mostly represent larger physical aspects of the world, like the region where a battle takes place, the types of holdings the player owns, or notable characters, as well as more abstract ideas, such as the guidance of a Kami or a specific event occurring.

Fate Cards are distinguished by their green background color on the back face of the card. Fate cards represent smaller physical items, but more often lean on the spiritual side of the game. Examples of these cards would be spells cast by magicians, weapons and ornamental jewelry, or actions that might occur while on the battlefield, as well as the titular five elemental rings.

Strongholds are the current base of the player. Strongholds are identified by their three icons along the side of the card, and in some cases, have a built in dial to count honor/dishonor.

Dynasty Deck

Dynasty Cards can be divided into the following subtypes:

  • Holdings: These produce the gold a player will spend on items, characters, and other cards. Most of the time, a holding produces gold, but some holdings have other effects as well, like an ability to gain honor.
  • Personalities: The player's notable personalities. These have a Force stat, comparable to strength; a Chi stat, like the force stat but only used for certain conditions; a Gold cost, Honor fan, and an honor requirement. A Personality can have multiple additional effects printed on the card as well.

    In order to bring a personality into play, a player must pay gold equal to their gold cost. The card must also be unaligned or of the same clan as the player's stronghold and the player must meet the honor requirement. Some of these requirements can be bypassed by spending additional gold, as well as the option to gain honor based on the honor icon on the personality.
  • Celestial Cards: These embody the guidance of a celestial being, and generally provide an effect either once they are revealed, or a permanent effect once in play.
  • Events: These are revealed in your provinces, and most often have an immediate effect. These can be wide ranging, from force modifiers, to honor gain, to other unique benefits.
  • Regions: These symbolize the terrain in a particular province, and attach to the province that they are revealed on. Generally they modify the province for combat, but can have other effects.
Fate Deck

Fate Cards can be divided into the following subtypes:

  • Actions: The most common type of fate cards. These have a one time effect, and are sometimes accompanied by a gold cost, or a specific cost attached to the effect of the card. The cost must be paid in order for the effect to be receieved.
  • Items: Items are equipped to specific personalities to give additional effects. In most cases, they provide a constant force or chi modifier. Most of the time, items come in the form of weapons, which most personalities can only have one. Duelists, however, can have two weapons equipped at a time.
  • Followers: Followers attach to a personality and provide a separate force of their own. Followers must be removed from a personality before the personality can be directly targeted by certain attacks. Followers have a gold cost and an honor requirement in some cases.
  • Spells: Spells are the embodiment on magic, generally used as a way to give repeatable actions in battle. The only personalities who can equip spells are the Shugenja, or spellcasters. The number of spells a Personality can equip is equal to their current Chi. Spells have a gold cost, similar to items and followers.
  • The Five Rings: The titular five elemental rings. Air, Earth, Fire, Water, and Void. Each ring can only be in play for one player at a time, and each ring has specific conditions to allow it to be put into play. Alternately, each ring can also be used singularly by discarding the card from your hand.
Winning the Game

The game can be won in a number of ways, and some cards provide a conditional win effect. There are four conditions set out by default in which a player can win:

Player 1 destroys all of the provinces of their opponent (by default, 4). Player 1 starts their turn with over 40 honor. Player 2 starts their turn with -20 or less honor. Player 1 plays all five elemental rings through their natural card text.

If any of these conditions are met in the above examples, player 1 will immediately win the game.

Turn Sequence

The turn sequence for the game is as follows:

  1. Straighten Phase: The active player straightens all of their cards that may have become bowed as a result of a card or battle.
  2. Events Phase: In this phase, a player flips over all face down dynasty cards. These are initially set up as four (4) face-down cards, and represent the individual physical land under the control of the player.Regions and events come into play immediately; personalities and holdings may be purchased later.
  3. Action Phase: Cards may be played out of hand, purchased with gold if needed. These include, Items, Followers, Actions, and Spells that might be in the player's hand, as well as possibly having additional abilities on their cards in play.
  4. Attack Phase: This is the only optional phase, in that players can choose to skip attacking entirely, while each other phase has a distinct step that must be followed every turn.
    In the attack phase, the active player can choose to attack any number of the opponent's provinces. They select any number of their personalities in play to attack, with the goal of a battle to be destroying the province. This is done only if the force total of the attacking army is greater than the province strength (indicated by a number on the Stronghold for that player) in addition to the force of any units the defending player assigns to that province.
  5. Dynasty Phase: In the Dynasty phase, the active player spends gold to purchase personalities and holdings from their provinces. Each face up personality or holding will have a printed gold cost that must be paid to bring then into play. This can be done by "bowing" a gold producing card, such as their stronghold or another holding. At the end of this phase, the active player draws a card from their Fate deck, to indicate their turn is completed.
In most phases, the active player will have the majority of actions available, while their opponent will have some actions, but not always many.
The Five Rings

The titular Five Rings of the game are based on the Five Elements of Rokugan: Air, Earth Fire, Water, and Void. Each of these rings has a specific condition that can be used to put the ring into play, as well as a means to get a beneficial effect by discarding. If a player is able to get all five rings into play by their printed card effect, that player is considered to have won an enlightenment victory, having mastered all of the natural elements.

Note that while it is possible to have rings enter play through other card effects, they will not count towards an enlightenment victory unless they enter play through their printed text.

Deck composition

Deck composition for a standard deck might look something like the following (Please note that these are just guidelines, and every deck will have different needs):

Dynasty Deck:

  • 16 Holdings
  • 20 Personalities
  • 2 Events
  • 2 Regions

Fate Deck:

  • 23 Strategies
  • 8 Items
  • 3 Followers
  • 3 Spells
  • 3 Rings
Honor and Dishonor

In the land of Rokugan, where the game takes place, a person's character and honor mattered in politics, as well as the public opinion of their clan. This is represented by the honor and dishonor system. There are numerour ways to gain honor, such as by paying two extra gold for a personality from your clan, on certain events, or as abilities on fate cards, personalities, holdings, etc.

On the opposing end, dishonor can greatly affect the reputation of a clan, should the dishonorable personality die. If a dishonored personality would ever be the cause of an honor gain, they are instead rehonored and the honor gain negated. If a dishonorable personlity dies, their player loses honor equal to their honor fan value (usually 0-4). When a player has less than -19 honor at the start of their turn, their opponent wins a dishonor victory.
